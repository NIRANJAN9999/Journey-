The journey

How I got here

  1. Where it started

    At the yearly fairs in my hometown — Mahankaali Jaathara, Shivaratri Jaathara — the toy every kid wanted was a remote-control car. I'd get one, play with it for a few days, then take it apart with a sharpener blade and a pair of scissors, since I didn't own a screwdriver yet. I wanted to see the motor, the gears, the wiring underneath. Putting it back together didn't always go well. My parents eventually stopped buying me new ones, so I started saving pocket money to buy my own.

  2. The first real build

    In 7th grade, I wired my new cycle with a hidden light — the battery tucked inside the seat, wires hidden along the frame, wired so the light only came on when I braked. The brake pads doubled as the switch: two contacts, touching the wheel rim, closing the circuit.

  3. Learning by breaking things

    A friend showed me how to spin an old motor fast enough to generate current — enough to light a small LED, rigged to my cycle wheel with whatever held it together. It worked, until the motor burned out from the speed. I opened it up, found the burned terminals, and replaced them with copper wire. It ran for a few more days after that. Around the same time, I wired a 12V LED straight into a wall socket, on the theory that voltage was voltage. It wasn't. I still remember that short circuit clearly — the first time I learned that electricity doesn't forgive guessing.

  4. Screens, then code

    When my phone's power button stopped working, I opened it with the same old blade — turned out the fix needed soldering, which I didn't have yet, so a repair shop finished the job. But taking it apart pulled me toward what was inside it in a different way: I started learning to code, edit photos and video, and run basic command-line tools on the phone itself. That was around 10th grade.

  5. A detour that wasn't wasted

    I got a mechanical engineering seat in college — not the computer science one I'd actually asked for. Three years in, I'd learned how gears and engines work, how to file and fit metal by hand, what a driveshaft and a universal joint actually do, and a little CAD. Old car-restoration shows kept me interested the whole way through. When the chance came to study computer science properly, I took it — but the mechanical years turned out to matter more than I expected at the time.

  6. Teaching myself the rest

    With a laptop of my own, I installed the Arduino IDE, bought a handful of microcontrollers, and learned mostly from YouTube — pasting code from video descriptions, running it, and slowly working out why it worked when it did and why it didn't when it didn't. ESP32 boards, LEDs, resistors, motor drivers. Around the same time, I fixed my own phone's battery, then a friend's, using nothing but tutorials and a soldering iron I'd bought myself.

  7. Full circle

    The car, finished

    At 20, I built the remote-control car I'd wanted to build since I was a kid — this time from scratch, driven from my own phone over Bluetooth. Same curiosity, fifteen years later, just with the skills to finish what I used to take apart.

What I'm doing at the moment

A day job outside my field, and real work in the evenings

A course toward an AI/ML role taught me a lot — the interview process taught me more, and not gently. About a year in, I stepped back. These days I work in documentation to cover rent, and the hours after that go to 3D printing, resin casting, and a lamp I'm building to sell.
